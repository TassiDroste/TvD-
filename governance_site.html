Performance audits

Topic 01
Environmental audit · Lake Chad Basin · 2015

A first for Africa: joint environmental audit of Lake Chad

Lake Chad has shrunk dramatically in recent decades — but the crisis was as much a governance failure as a climate one. Poor water resource management, fragmented oversight, and absent accountability mechanisms allowed the problem to deepen even as the human costs escalated: livelihoods destroyed, communities displaced, a vacuum filled by extremism.

Working under the auspices of AFROSAI with GIZ support, the Supreme Audit Institutions of Nigeria, Niger, Chad and Cameroon jointly evaluated the effectiveness of water use controls and the application of regulations across all relevant government departments. The audit report was presented to President Idriss Déby in May 2015 — the first joint environmental audit ever submitted to authorities on the African continent.

The process showed that performance auditing can serve as a practical tool for cross-border accountability on shared natural resources — and that SAIs, with targeted capacity support, can address the deeper governance failures that drive instability, not just financial irregularities.

"Lack of water, poverty, limited economic perspectives are a fertile ground for all kinds of vulnerabilities."
— German Ambassador Helmut Kulitz, at the handover ceremony to President Déby, N'Djamena, May 2015

Citizen engagement & digital governance

Topic 02
Civic tech · Ghana & Uganda · 2018–2025

CitizensEye and the Citizen Feedback Platform: turning citizens into auditors

People complain about poor services — broken roads, absent teachers, leaking water systems — yet rarely engage with the institutions that could fix them. This disengagement erodes trust in public authorities and, over time, the social fabric itself. The question was: could a digital tool bridge this gap and make citizen feedback genuinely useful for accountability?

In 2018, working with the Ghana Audit Service and GIZ's innovation fund, the CitizensEye app was developed and launched — the first tool of its kind for a Supreme Audit Institution in Africa. Like TripAdvisor for public services, it allowed Ghanaians to rate schools, hospitals and infrastructure, report geolocated problems such as potholes or corruption, and send feedback directly to the Auditor General for use in audit planning.

The model was then adapted for Uganda's Office of the Auditor General through the Citizen Feedback Platform (CFP), developed under the Strengthening Governance and Civil Society Programme. Partnerships between civil society organisations — particularly the Civil Society Budget Advocacy Group (CSBAG) — and the OAG enabled 495 citizens, 76% of them youth, to actively report service delivery issues. By 2024, they had submitted 726 reports from 20 local governments, covering road failures, water access gaps, and school infrastructure deficits.

"Duty bearers are now more cautious in their work, as they see us almost like auditors."
— Gonansa Emmanuel, Participatory Budget Club Member, Budaka District, Uganda

Inclusive governance

Topic 03
Civil society & inclusive policy · Uganda · 2019–2025

Ensuring no one is left behind: youth, women, and persons with disabilities in governance

Governance cannot be good if the people most affected by public services have no voice in shaping them. Uganda has one of the youngest populations in the world — over 78% of citizens are under 30 — yet youth, women, persons with disabilities, and refugees remain systematically underrepresented in policy-making, budget processes, and accountability mechanisms.

The Civil Society in Uganda Support Programme (CUSP), co-funded by the EU and Germany and managed under the Strengthening Governance and Civil Society Programme, was built around a simple premise: that meaningful participation is not optional for good governance, it is its precondition. Through partnerships with civil society organisations across all regions of Uganda, CUSP supported youth parliaments, vocational centres for persons with disabilities, sport-based empowerment programmes for girls, AI innovation hackathons for CSOs, and digital planning tools for local governments serving refugee communities.

By 2024, seven sub-national youth parliaments had mobilised 310 young people, feeding resolutions into national debates on economic policy, electoral reform, and corruption. An EU Youth Sounding Board gave 20 young Ugandans a direct advisory role with the EU Delegation. And the HEVOT vocational centre, built through CUSP support, opened in 2024 to provide skills training specifically designed for young people with disabilities.

"Shaping policies and driving societal change should not only be done with young people in mind but pushed for and brought about by them."
— Tassilo von Droste, Head of Programme, CUSP Bulletin Vol. 3, January 2025

Essay · Governance & development

Why governance is not
a technical problem —
it is a moral one

On power, accountability, and what it means to build a state that works for everyone

Tassilo von Droste zu Hülshoff
18 years in development cooperation · Uganda, South Africa, Chad, Ghana
Governance

Governance is one of those words that has been stretched so far it has nearly lost its shape. Donors use it to justify conditionalities. Governments invoke it to signal reform intent. Academics write entire careers around its definition. And yet, stripped of jargon, it is a simple idea: governance is about whether state power is used for the benefit of citizens at large, or for the interests of the connected few.

That simplicity matters. Because when governance fails, it is not an abstraction that suffers — it is people. Children sitting in classrooms without textbooks. Patients in health centres without medicines. Bridges that collapse because procurement contracts were awarded on the basis of connections rather than competence. Roads left unrepaired not because funds were unavailable, but because those funds never reached their destination.

"The question is not whether a government governs, but whether it governs for everyone."

What good governance actually means

Good governance has many dimensions — transparency, accountability, participation, the rule of law, efficiency, equity. It is sometimes taught as a checklist, and that is where things go wrong. Governance is not a list of institutional boxes to tick. It is a set of relationships: between the state and its citizens, between public officials and the law, between those with power and those affected by it.

At its core, good governance requires three things. First, solid mechanisms for policy development — processes that are inclusive, evidence-based, and responsive to the needs of all citizens, including those who are easiest to overlook. Second, effective implementation — the administrative capacity to translate policy into services, and the management culture to do so honestly. Third, and perhaps most importantly, credible accountability — institutions that are genuinely independent, that can investigate, report, and where necessary, sanction. Supreme Audit Institutions, human rights commissions, independent judiciaries: these are not bureaucratic niceties. They are the mechanisms through which citizens verify that power is being used on their behalf.

The independence of these institutions is not a procedural detail. It is the difference between a state that can be held to account and one that cannot. When an Auditor General can be removed by the executive for inconvenient findings, or when a human rights body is starved of funding, the entire architecture of accountability is hollowed out. What remains is the form of governance without its substance.

The challenge of inclusion
No one should be left behind — but this requires deliberate design
Inclusive governance does not happen by default. Marginalised groups — women, persons with disabilities, youth, refugees, rural communities — are systematically underrepresented in the processes that shape the services they depend on most. Building systems that cannot be misused through the abuse of power means building systems that actively create equal opportunities and structure participation so that those with the least voice have mechanisms to be heard.

Democracy between elections

When we think about democracy, we think of elections. But elections take place every five years. In between, there are thousands of days during which government decisions are made, budgets are spent, contracts are awarded, and services either reach people or they do not. Democracy is not only the moment of the vote — it is everything that happens in between.

This is where citizen engagement becomes critical, and where its traditional forms fall short. Participation in budget hearings requires literacy, time, and proximity to district offices. Filing a complaint with an oversight body requires knowledge of how to do so and confidence that something will happen. For most citizens — particularly those in rural areas, or those from marginalised groups — these pathways are effectively closed.

Digital tools have the potential to change this. Not as a silver bullet, but as a genuine leveller — lowering the cost of participation, making technical processes accessible, and creating channels through which ordinary citizens can contribute to accountability in real time. The CitizensEye app developed for Ghana's Audit Service, and the Citizen Feedback Platform now used by Uganda's Office of the Auditor General, are examples of what this looks like in practice: citizens reporting geolocated service failures directly to auditors, generating evidence that shapes what gets audited and where public attention is directed.

Artificial intelligence and the future of governance

Digitalisation offers something that previous governance reforms could not: the possibility of evidence-based decision-making at scale, in real time, based on the actual behaviour of systems and citizens rather than the assumptions of planners sitting in capital cities.

Consider a practical example. Traffic management in Kampala. An AI system analysing vehicle flow and road conditions across the city could tell the Kampala Capital City Authority not just where potholes exist, but which ones are driven over most frequently — the ones whose repair would reduce the most damage to the most vehicles, for the most people, every single day. Every shilling of public maintenance spending invested where it has the greatest impact on the greatest number of people. A utilitarian calculus in the best sense — maximising social welfare from constrained public resources, in the spirit of what Locke articulated about the purpose of political authority: to serve the governed.

The same logic applies to hospital allocation, to school infrastructure, to water access. Data — properly collected, properly disaggregated — allows planners to understand not just where need exists in aggregate, but where investment would have the greatest measurable effect on human outcomes. It can prevent public money from flowing disproportionately to areas that are already well-served, and redirect it to where it would do most good.

But disaggregation matters enormously here. Aggregate data conceals inequality. An analysis of crime across a city might suggest that street lighting is needed in certain commercial districts. But if the objective is to protect women and children from gender-based violence, the relevant data is different — where crimes against these groups have occurred, at what times, in what conditions. The tool is only as equitable as the question it is asked to answer.

Road maintenance prioritisation
AI analysis of traffic frequency and road condition data to identify the repairs that serve the most people — directing limited maintenance budgets where they have the greatest impact.
Healthcare facility siting
Using population health data to model where an additional clinic would have the largest effect on life expectancy and access — not where it is politically convenient.
Street lighting & safety
Disaggregated crime data, analysed by victim group and location, to direct safety infrastructure where it most protects women and children.
Budget accessibility
AI summarisation of complex budget and audit documents — making technical accountability processes legible for citizens and civil society without specialist training.

The risks we cannot ignore

None of this is inevitable. Digitalisation does not automatically produce better governance — it amplifies the governance that exists. In the hands of accountable, citizen-oriented institutions, digital tools and AI can be transformative instruments of equity. In the hands of unaccountable ones, they can become instruments of surveillance, exclusion, and control.

Opportunities
Real-time citizen feedback on service delivery
Evidence-based resource allocation for greatest social impact
AI-powered access to complex budget and audit processes
Disaggregated data to protect and include marginalised groups
Lower barriers to civic participation for rural communities
Risks
State surveillance and curtailment of individual rights
Data used against citizens rather than for them
AI bias reflecting and amplifying existing inequalities
Private platforms capturing public data without accountability
Digital exclusion deepening the gap for those already left behind

Good governance of digitalisation — including the ethical governance of AI — is therefore not a separate agenda from good governance more broadly. It is its latest chapter. The same principles apply: transparency, accountability, independence, inclusion. Data must be governed with the same rigour as public finances. Algorithms used in public decision-making must be subject to audit. Citizens must have the right to understand how decisions that affect them are made, and the means to challenge them.

More cooperation, not less

We are living through a moment of acute pressure on the institutions and norms that underpin international cooperation. Belligerent nationalism is on the rise. Multilateral frameworks are under strain. The temptation in many capitals is to turn inward — to treat governance as a domestic matter, immune from external scrutiny or solidarity.

This is precisely the wrong response. The challenges that matter most — climate change, forced displacement, transnational corruption, the governance of AI — cannot be addressed by any single state acting alone. They require the kind of sustained cooperation that only robust international institutions can provide. And they require domestic governance frameworks strong enough to resist capture by narrow interests.

We have learned, through hard experience across many countries and many decades, that open societies do not sustain themselves automatically. They require active maintenance: independent oversight institutions, a free press, civil society organisations with the space to operate, and the political will to protect all of these when they come under pressure. The erosion of any one of them weakens all the others.

The goal is a society in which technology strengthens democracy rather than hollowing it out — where the distance between a citizen and the institutions that serve them is reduced, not by removing accountability but by making it more immediate, more legible, and more real.

"Good governance is ultimately an act of collective empathy — a society's decision that the wellbeing of the least powerful matters as much as the wellbeing of the most."
This is the conviction that has shaped eighteen years of work across Africa — in audit institutions, in civil society, in programme management, and in the design of tools that put accountability in citizens' hands.
About

TvD

I have spent eighteen years trying to answer a deceptively simple question: how do you build a state that actually serves its citizens? Not a state on paper — one that works in practice, for the farmer in northern Uganda, for the woman in a Chadian village where the lake is drying up, for the young person in Kampala who wants to know that the road outside their school will be fixed.

That question has taken me from Mauritania to Brussels, from Pretoria to N'Djamena, and most recently to Kampala, where I managed the Strengthening Governance and Civil Society Programme — a €44.7 million initiative co-funded by Germany and the European Union, working with civil society organisations, oversight institutions, and government across all regions of Uganda.

My career began in public financial management and audit — unglamorous work, but foundational. You cannot have good governance without institutions that can follow the money: Supreme Audit Institutions that are genuinely independent, parliaments with real oversight capacity, budget processes that are open to scrutiny. I spent years supporting exactly this across Africa, advising the African Organisation of Supreme Audit Institutions (AFROSAI) and leading GIZ's Good Financial Governance programme in Pretoria.

Over time, I became increasingly convinced that accountability without citizen engagement is incomplete. Audit reports sit on shelves. Findings go unacted upon. The missing link is an informed, empowered citizenry that can demand change. That conviction led me to develop CitizensEye in 2018 — the first citizen feedback app for a Supreme Audit Institution in Africa, launched with Ghana's Audit Service — and later to oversee the development of Uganda's Citizen Feedback Platform, which has put accountability tools directly in the hands of communities across twenty local governments.

Inclusive governance is the third thread running through my work. Accountability means little if it only works for the already-powerful. The programmes I have managed have prioritised the participation of youth, women, persons with disabilities, and other marginalised groups — not as a box to tick, but because governance that excludes them is, by definition, not good governance.

"We have learned, through hard experience, that open societies, international cooperation, and robust independent institutions are not luxuries — they are what stand between citizens and the abuse of power."

I write and think about these issues not as an academic but as a practitioner who has seen what works and what does not — across very different contexts, under very different conditions. The current moment, marked by a resurgence of belligerent nationalism and a retreat from multilateralism, makes this work more urgent, not less. The temptation to turn inward, to weaken international institutions, to treat governance as a domestic affair immune from external scrutiny — these are precisely the conditions under which accountability erodes and citizens pay the price.

More cooperation, not less. Stronger institutions, not weaker ones. That is the lesson of the past decades, and the conviction that continues to drive my work.
